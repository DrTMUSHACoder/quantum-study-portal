1. Consider the $N$-qubit computational basis state

$$
|X\rangle = |X_{N-1}X_{N-2}\dots X_1X_0\rangle.
$$

We are given

$$
|\psi\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2^N}} \sum_{Y \in \{0,1\}^N} (-1)^{X \cdot Y} |Y\rangle,
$$

where the mod-2 inner product is

$$
X \cdot Y = (X_{N-1}Y_{N-1}) \oplus (X_{N-2}Y_{N-2}) \oplus \dots \oplus (X_0Y_0).
$$

## Step 1: Action of the Hadamard Transform

Recall the single-qubit Hadamard action:

$$
H|0\rangle = \frac{|0\rangle + |1\rangle}{\sqrt{2}}, \qquad H|1\rangle = \frac{|0\rangle - |1\rangle}{\sqrt{2}}.
$$

For $N$ qubits, the tensor product gives the identity:

$$
H^{\otimes N}|X\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2^N}} \sum_{Y \in \{0,1\}^N} (-1)^{X \cdot Y} |Y\rangle.
$$

Comparing with the given expression, we see immediately that

$$
|\psi\rangle = H^{\otimes N}|X\rangle.
$$

Hence the required unitary is

$$
\boxed{\hat{U} = H^{\otimes N}.}
$$

## Step 2: Recovering $X$

Since the Hadamard transform is its own inverse,

$$
H^{\otimes N}H^{\otimes N} = I,
$$

--- Page 1 ---

applying  $H^{\otimes N}$  again gives

$$
H ^ {\otimes N} | \psi \rangle = H ^ {\otimes N} H ^ {\otimes N} | X \rangle = | X \rangle .
$$

Measuring in the computational basis then yields  $X$  with probability 1.

Final Answer:

(c)  $\hat{U} = H^{\otimes N}$ , and  $X$  can be perfectly recovered.

2. In the Deutsch-Jozsa problem, a function  $f: \{0,1\}^n \to \{0,1\}$  is promised to be either constant or balanced.

To determine with  $100\%$  certainty whether the function is constant or balanced using a classical deterministic algorithm:

# Worst-case analysis:

A balanced function outputs 0 for exactly  $2^{n - 1}$  inputs and 1 for the other  $2^{n - 1}$  inputs. In the worst case, the algorithm may query  $2^{n - 1}$  inputs and obtain the same output each time. At this stage, the function could still be constant, or it could be balanced (with the opposite value appearing in the remaining inputs).

Therefore, one additional query is required to break the ambiguity.

Hence, the minimum number of queries required is

$$
2 ^ {n - 1} + 1.
$$

Final Answer:

(c)  $2^{n - 1} + 1$

4. Both the Deutsch-Jozsa and Bernstein-Vazirani algorithms encode the function output  $f(x)$  into the phase of the quantum state.

# Key properties exploited:

- Superposition: The input register is prepared in an equal superposition of all possible  $x$ , allowing the function to be evaluated on all inputs simultaneously.
- Phase Kickback: Using a suitably prepared ancilla qubit, the function value  $f(x)$  is transferred into the phase factor  $(-1)^{f(x)}$  of the computational basis states.

The Uncertainty Principle is not directly used in this encoding process.

# Final Answer:

(b) Phase Kickback and (c) Superposition.

10. In Grover’s algorithm, the state evolution can be understood as a rotation in a two-dimensional subspace of the full Hilbert space.

Geometric Interpretation:

The relevant 2D plane is spanned by:

- The target state $|a\rangle$,
- The state $|e\rangle$, which is the uniform superposition of all non-target states.

These two states are orthogonal and form a basis for the subspace in which Grover’s iterations perform successive rotations toward $|a\rangle$.

Final Answer:

$(b)$ The target state $|a\rangle$ and a uniform superposition of all non-target states, $|e\rangle$.
