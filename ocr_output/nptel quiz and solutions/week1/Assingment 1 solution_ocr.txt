1. Recall the Bloch sphere (geometric) representation of a qubit system, wherein the state of a qubit is parameterized by two angles $\theta$ and $\phi$, as

$$
| \psi \rangle = \left(\cos \frac {\theta}{2}\right) | 0 \rangle + e ^ {i \phi} \left(\sin \frac {\theta}{2}\right) | 1 \rangle .
$$

What are the values of the angles $\theta$ and $\phi$ for the state $|\psi \rangle = \left( \begin{array}{l} (1 + i) / \sqrt{2} \\ (1 - i) / \sqrt{2} \end{array} \right)$?

Step 1: Normalization

$$
\left| \frac {1 + i}{\sqrt {2}} \right| ^ {2} + \left| \frac {1 - i}{\sqrt {2}} \right| ^ {2} = 1 + 1 = 2 \neq 1.
$$

Hence, the state is not normalized. Then normalize the state by $1 / \sqrt{2}$ factor.

Step 2: Removal of global phase

Factoring out the global phase $e^{i\pi /4}$, we obtain

$$
| \psi \rangle = \frac {1}{\sqrt {2}} e ^ {i \pi / 4} \left( \begin{array}{c} 1 \\ - i \end{array} \right).
$$

Since

$$
- 1 = e ^ {- i \pi / 2},
$$

Dropping the physically irrelevant global phase $e^{i\pi /4}$, we have

$$
| \psi \rangle = \frac {1}{\sqrt {2}} \left( \begin{array}{c} 1 \\ e ^ {- i \pi / 2} \end{array} \right).
$$

Step 3: Identification of Bloch sphere angles

Comparing with

$$
| \psi \rangle = \cos \left(\frac {\theta}{2}\right) | 0 \rangle + e ^ {i \phi} \sin \left(\frac {\theta}{2}\right) | 1 \rangle ,
$$

we identify

$$
\cos \left(\frac {\theta}{2}\right) = \frac {1}{\sqrt {2}} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \frac {\theta}{2} = \frac {\pi}{4} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \theta = \frac {\pi}{2}.
$$

The relative phase gives

$$
e ^ {i \phi} = e ^ {- i \pi / 2} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \phi = \frac {3 \pi}{2},
$$

where $0\leq \phi &lt; 2\pi$

Final Answer:

$$
\boxed {\theta = \frac {\pi}{2}, \qquad \phi = \frac {3 \pi}{2}}
$$

2. The state $|\phi \rangle = \left(\frac{1 + i}{4}\right)|0\rangle +\left(\frac{3 - i}{4}\right)|1\rangle$ is measured in the $\{|+ \rangle, |- \rangle\}$ basis (also called the Hadamard basis). What is the probability of obtaining $|+ \rangle$ as the measurement result? Step 1: Check normalization

$$
\left| \frac {1 + i}{4} \right| ^ {2} = \frac {1 ^ {2} + 1 ^ {2}}{1 6} = \frac {2}{1 6} = \frac {1}{8},
$$

$$
\left| \frac {3 - i}{4} \right| ^ {2} = \frac {3 ^ {2} + 1 ^ {2}}{1 6} = \frac {1 0}{1 6} = \frac {5}{8}.
$$

$$
\frac {1}{8} + \frac {5}{8} = \frac {6}{8} \neq 1.
$$

Hence the state is not normalized. The normalization factor is

$$
\sqrt {\frac {6}{8}} = \sqrt {\frac {3}{4}} = \frac {\sqrt {3}}{2}.
$$

The normalized state is therefore

$$
| \phi \rangle = \frac {1}{\sqrt {3}} \left[ \left(\frac {1 + i}{2}\right) | 0 \rangle + \left(\frac {3 - i}{2}\right) | 1 \rangle \right].
$$

Step 2: Express $|+ \rangle$ bra

$$
\langle + | = \frac {1}{\sqrt {2}} \left(\langle 0 | + \langle 1 |\right).
$$

Step 3: Compute the probability amplitude

$$
\begin{array}{l}
\langle + | \phi \rangle = \frac {1}{\sqrt {2}} (\langle 0 | + \langle 1 |) \frac {1}{\sqrt {3}} \left[ \left(\frac {1 + i}{2}\right) | 0 \rangle + \left(\frac {3 - i}{2}\right) | 1 \rangle \right]. \\
= \frac {1}{\sqrt {6}} \left[ \frac {1 + i}{2} + \frac {3 - i}{2} \right]. \\
= \frac {1}{\sqrt {6}} \cdot \frac {(1 + i + 3 - i)}{2} = \frac {1}{\sqrt {6}} \cdot \frac {4}{2} = \frac {2}{\sqrt {6}}.
$$

$P(+)=|\langle+|\phi\rangle|^{2}=\left(\frac{2}{\sqrt{6}}\right)^{2}=\frac{4}{6}=\boxed{\frac{2}{3}}.$
3. Which of the following gate sequences adds a relative phase of $\pi$ between $|0\rangle$ and $|1\rangle$ ? A gate sequence adds a relative phase of $\pi$ between $|0\rangle$ and $|1\rangle$ if, for an arbitrary input

$|\psi_{\rm in}\rangle=\alpha\,|0\rangle+\beta\,|1\rangle\,,$

the overall unitary is equivalent (up to a global phase) to the Pauli-$Z$ gate,

\[ Z=\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\
0&-1\end{pmatrix}, \]

which maps $|\psi_{\rm in}\rangle$ to

$Z\,|\psi_{\rm in}\rangle=\alpha\,|0\rangle-\beta\,|1\rangle\,.$

This introduces a relative phase of $\pi$ between the amplitudes of $|0\rangle$ and $|1\rangle$.

(b) Circuit: $ZSSSS$

Using $S^{2}=Z$ and $Z^{2}=I$,

$S^{2}=Z,\qquad S^{4}=(S^{2})^{2}=Z^{2}=I,\qquad ZS^{4}=Z.$

Thus the entire sequence $SSSS$ is equivalent to a single $I$ gate:

$Z\,|\psi_{\rm in}\rangle=\alpha|0\rangle-\beta|1\rangle.$

A relative phase of $\pi$. Any sequence with an even number of $S$ gates acts as $Z$ (up to a global phase).

(c) Circuit: $XYZZ$

First simplify the product using Pauli algebra.

$Z^{2}=I\ \Rightarrow\ XYZZ=XYI=XY.$

The Pauli matrices satisfy

$XY=iZ.$

Hence the full unitary is

$U_{c}=XYZZ=iZ.$

Up to a global phase $i$, this is exactly the $Z$ gate. Acting on $|\psi_{\rm in}\rangle$ gives

$U_{c}\,|\psi_{\rm in}\rangle=iZ\,|\psi_{\rm in}\rangle=i(\alpha\,|0\rangle-\beta\,|1\rangle),$

which is globally equivalent to $\alpha\,|0\rangle-\beta\,|1\rangle$. Therefore this sequence introduces a relative phase of $\pi$ between $|0\rangle$ and $|1\rangle$.

(a) Circuit: $HXS$

Compute the overall unitary:

$S=\begin{pmatrix}1&0\cr 0&i\end{pmatrix},\qquad XS=\begin{pmatrix}0&1\cr 1&0\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}1&0\cr 0&i\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}0&i\cr 1&0\end{pmatrix},$

$U_{a}=HXS=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}1&1\cr 1&-1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}0&i\cr 1&0\end{pmatrix}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}1&i\cr-1&i\end{pmatrix}.$

Act on the basis states:

$U_{a}\ket{0}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{0}-\ket{1}),\qquad U_{a}\ket{1}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(i\ket{0}+i\ket{1})=i\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{0}+\ket{1}).$

This is not simply $\ket{0}\mapsto\ket{0}$, $\ket{1}\mapsto-\ket{1}$ up to a common phase. The transformation mixes $\ket{0}$ and $\ket{1}$, so it does *not* implement a pure relative phase of $\pi$ in the computational basis.

(d) Circuit: $HXY$

Simplify the product:

$XY=iZ\quad\Rightarrow\quad U_{d}=HXY=H(iZ)=iHZ.$

Up to a global phase, the unitary is $HZ$. On the basis states,

$Z\ket{0}=\ket{0},\quad Z\ket{1}=-\ket{1},$
$HZ\ket{0}=H\ket{0}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{0}+\ket{1}),$
$HZ\ket{1}=H(-\ket{1})=-\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\ket{0}-\ket{1}).$

Again, the computational basis states are mapped to superpositions, so this is not a pure phase gate on $\{\ket{0},\ket{1}\}$.

4. Suppose the state $\ket{+}=\frac{\ket{0}+\ket{1}}{\sqrt{2}}$, is transformed via a quantum gate of the form $P=(\ket{0}\!\bra{0}+e^{i\phi}(\ket{1}\!\bra{1}$. Find the angle $\phi$ such that, after the action of the gate, the probability of obtaining $\ket{+}$ is $1/2$.

$P\ket{0}=\ket{0},\qquad P\ket{1}=e^{i\phi}\ket{1}.$

Applying $P$ to $\ket{+}$ gives

$\ket{\psi}=P\ket{+}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\big{(}P\ket{0}+P\ket{1}\big{)}=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\big{(}\ket{0}+e^{i\phi}\ket{1}\big{)}.$

The probability of obtaining $\ket{+}$ upon measurement in the $\{\ket{+},\ket{-}\}$ basis is

$p_{+}=\ket{\bra{+}\!\ket{\psi}}^{2}.$

Since

$\bra{+}=\frac{\bra{0}+\bra{1}}{\sqrt{2}},$

we have

$\langle+|\psi\rangle=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(\langle 0|+\langle 1|)\cdot\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|0\rangle+e^{i\phi}\,|1\rangle)=\frac{1}{2}(1+e^{i\phi}).$

Thus

$p_{+}=\left|\frac{1+e^{i\phi}}{2}\right|^{2}=\frac{1}{4}|1+e^{i\phi}|^{2}.$

Now

$|1+e^{i\phi}|^{2}=(1+e^{i\phi})(1+e^{-i\phi})=2+2\cos\phi=4\cos^{2}\left(\frac{\phi}{2}\right),$

so

$p_{+}=\frac{1}{4}\cdot 4\cos^{2}\left(\frac{\phi}{2}\right)=\cos^{2}\left(\frac{\phi}{2}\right).$

We require $p_{+}=\frac{1}{2}$, hence

$\cos^{2}\left(\frac{\phi}{2}\right)=\frac{1}{2}\quad\Rightarrow\quad\left|\cos\left(\frac{\phi}{2}\right)\right|=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}.$

Therefore

$\frac{\phi}{2}=\frac{\pi}{4}+k\frac{\pi}{2}\quad\Rightarrow\quad\phi=\frac{\pi}{2}+k\pi,\qquad k\in\mathbb{Z}.$

From the given options, the values satisfying the condition are $\phi=\frac{\pi}{2}$.

6. Which of these are valid quantum gates?

A matrix represents a valid single-qubit quantum gate iff it is unitary, i.e. $U^{\dagger}U=I$.

(a) $U_{a}$ is diagonal with entries of modulus $1$, hence

\[ U_{a}^{\dagger}U_{a}=\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\
0&e^{-i\pi/4}\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\
0&e^{i\pi/4}\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\
0&1\end{pmatrix}=I, \]

so $U_{a}$ is unitary.

(b) Columns of $U_b$ are

$$
c_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1/\sqrt{2} \\ 1/\sqrt{2} \end{pmatrix}, \qquad c_2 = \begin{pmatrix} -i/\sqrt{2} \\ i/\sqrt{2} \end{pmatrix}.
$$

Norms:

$$
\|c_1\|^2 = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2} = 1, \qquad \|c_2\|^2 = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2} = 1.
$$

Inner product:

$$
c_1^\dagger c_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1, 1) \begin{pmatrix} -i/\sqrt{2} \\ i/\sqrt{2} \end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{2}(-i + i) = 0.
$$

The columns are orthonormal, hence $U_b^\dagger U_b = I$ and $U_b$ is unitary.

(c) Columns:

$$
c_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1/\sqrt{2} \\ 1/\sqrt{2} \end{pmatrix}, \qquad c_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ e^{i\pi/4} \end{pmatrix}.
$$

Norms: $\|c_1\|^2 = 1$, $\|c_2\|^2 = |e^{i\pi/4}|^2 = 1$. Inner product:

$$
c_1^\dagger c_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1, 1) \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ e^{i\pi/4} \end{pmatrix} = \frac{e^{i\pi/4}}{\sqrt{2}} \neq 0.
$$

Columns are not orthogonal, so $U_c$ is not unitary and therefore not a valid gate.

(d) Again diagonal with unit-modulus entries:

$$
U_d^\dagger U_d = \begin{pmatrix} e^{i\pi/3} &amp; 0 \\ 0 &amp; e^{-i\pi/3} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} e^{-i\pi/3} &amp; 0 \\ 0 &amp; e^{i\pi/3} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 &amp; 0 \\ 0 &amp; 1 \end{pmatrix} = I.
$$

Hence $U_d$ is unitary.

Therefore the valid quantum gates are $\boxed{(a), (b), \text{ and } (d)}$.

7. Which of these circuits implement the transformation $|00\rangle \rightarrow \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} |00\rangle - \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} |11\rangle$?

Start in the state $|00\rangle$. Apply an $X$ gate on the first qubit:

$$
(X \otimes I) |00\rangle = |10\rangle.
$$

Apply a Hadamard gate $H$ on the first qubit:

$$
(H \otimes I) |10\rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|00\rangle - |10\rangle).
$$

Apply a CNOT gate with the first qubit as control and the second as target. The CNOT action is

$$
|00\rangle \mapsto |00\rangle, \qquad |10\rangle \mapsto |11\rangle.
$$

Therefore

$$
\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|00\rangle - |10\rangle) \xrightarrow{\text{CNOT}} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|00\rangle - |11\rangle) = |\Phi^-\rangle.
$$

9. Which of the following states are equivalent to $|\psi \rangle = \cos \frac{\pi}{4} |0\rangle + e^{i\pi /3}\sin \frac{\pi}{4} |1\rangle$ physically?

Two pure states $|\phi \rangle$ and $|\psi \rangle$ are physically equivalent iff $|\phi \rangle = e^{i\alpha}|\psi \rangle$ for some real $\alpha$, i.e. they differ only by a global phase.

**Option (a).** $|\psi_{a}\rangle = e^{i\frac{\pi}{2}}\cos \frac{\pi}{4} |0\rangle +e^{i\left(\frac{\pi}{2} +\frac{\pi}{3}\right)}\sin \frac{\pi}{4} |1\rangle .$

Factor out $e^{i\frac{\pi}{2}}$:

$$
| \psi_ {a} \rangle = e ^ {i \frac {\pi}{2}} \left(\cos \frac {\pi}{4} | 0 \rangle + e ^ {i \frac {\pi}{3}} \sin \frac {\pi}{4} | 1 \rangle\right) = e ^ {i \frac {\pi}{2}} | \psi \rangle .
$$

Thus $|\psi_{a}\rangle$ is physically equivalent to $|\psi \rangle$.

**Option (c).** $|\psi_c\rangle = -\cos \frac{\pi}{4} |0\rangle -e^{i\frac{\pi}{3}}\sin \frac{\pi}{4} |1\rangle = -\left(\cos \frac{\pi}{4} |0\rangle +e^{i\frac{\pi}{3}}\sin \frac{\pi}{4} |1\rangle\right) = -|\psi \rangle = e^{i\pi}|\psi \rangle .$

Again, this is just a global phase, so $|\psi_c\rangle$ is physically equivalent to $|\psi \rangle$.

**Options (b) and (d).** For $|\psi \rangle$, the ratio of amplitudes is

$$
\frac {\mathrm {a m p} (1)}{\mathrm {a m p} (0)} = e ^ {i \frac {\pi}{3}}.
$$

For option (b),

$$
\frac {\mathrm {a m p} (1)}{\mathrm {a m p} (0)} = e ^ {i \frac {4 \pi}{3}},
$$

and for option (d),

$$
\frac {\mathrm {a m p} (1)}{\mathrm {a m p} (0)} = e ^ {i \frac {\pi}{6}}.
$$

Neither $e^{i\frac{4\pi}{3}}$ nor $e^{i\frac{\pi}{6}}$ equals $e^{i\frac{\pi}{3}}$, so there is no single global phase relating $|\psi\rangle$ to $|\psi_b\rangle$ or $|\psi_d\rangle$.

Therefore, the states in (a) and (c) are physically equivalent to $|\psi \rangle$, while those in (b) and (d) are not.
